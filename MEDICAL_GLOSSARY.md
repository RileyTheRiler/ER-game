# CODE BLUE: Medical Terminology Glossary

## How This Glossary Works

Terms are unlocked as you encounter them in gameplay. Each entry includes:
- **Definition**: Plain-language explanation
- **Context**: When you'll hear/use this
- **Related Terms**: Connected concepts
- **CPC Note**: Coding relevance (when applicable)

# SECTION A: BASIC TERMINOLOGY

## Vital Signs

### Heart Rate (HR) / Pulse
**Definition**: Number of heartbeats per minute.
**Normal Range**: Adults 60-100 BPM
**Context**: Checked on every patient, part of triage.
**Abnormal**:
- Tachycardia: HR > 100 (fast)
- Bradycardia: HR < 60 (slow)
**Clinical Pearl**: Fever increases HR by ~10 BPM per degree.

### Blood Pressure (BP)
**Definition**: Force of blood against artery walls. Written as systolic/diastolic.
**Normal Range**: Adults 120/80 mmHg (approximate)
**Context**: Elevated in pain, stress, stroke. Low in shock, bleeding.
**Abnormal**:
- Hypertension: Sustained >140/90
- Hypotension: <90/60 or symptomatic drop
- Shock: "BP 80 palp" means only systolic detected by palpation
**CPC Note**: Essential hypertension (I10), Hypotension (I95.9)

### Respiratory Rate (RR)
**Definition**: Breaths per minute.
**Normal Range**: Adults 12-20
**Context**: Often the first vital sign to change when patient is decompensating.
**Abnormal**:
- Tachypnea: RR > 20 (fast, shallow)
- Bradypnea: RR < 12 (slow)
- Kussmaul: Deep, rapid breathing (DKA)

### Oxygen Saturation (O2 Sat / SpO2)
**Definition**: Percentage of hemoglobin carrying oxygen.
**Normal Range**: 95-100% on room air
**Context**: Measured by pulse oximeter on finger.
**Abnormal**:
- <90% = Hypoxemia, needs supplemental O2
- <80% = Severe, approaching emergency
**Clinical Pearl**: CO poisoning can show falsely normal SpO2.

### Temperature
**Definition**: Body temperature.
**Normal Range**: 97.8-99.1°F (36.5-37.3°C)
**Context**: Fever suggests infection or inflammation.
**Abnormal**:
- Fever: >100.4°F (38°C)
- Hypothermia: <95°F (35°C)
- Hyperthermia: Dangerously elevated without infection
**CPC Note**: Fever of unknown origin (R50.9)

## Triage Terms

### Acuity
**Definition**: Severity/urgency of a patient's condition.
**Context**: Determines prioritization and room placement.
**Levels (ESI - Emergency Severity Index)**:
- ESI 1: Resuscitation (dying, code)
- ESI 2: Emergent (potential threat to life)
- ESI 3: Urgent (multiple resources needed)
- ESI 4: Less urgent (one resource)
- ESI 5: Non-urgent (no resources)

### Chief Complaint (CC)
**Definition**: The main reason the patient came to the ER, in their own words.
**Context**: First thing documented, guides initial assessment.
**Example**: "Chest pain," "Shortness of breath," "I fell"

### History of Present Illness (HPI)
**Definition**: Detailed story of the current problem.
**Context**: The narrative you build from patient interview.
**Elements** (OLDCARTS or OPQRST mnemonics):
- Onset, Location, Duration, Character, Aggravating/Alleviating factors, Radiation, Timing, Severity

### Past Medical History (PMH)
**Definition**: Patient's previous diagnoses and conditions.
**Context**: Critical for understanding baseline and risks.
**Common entries**: HTN, DM, CAD, COPD, etc.

### Allergies
**Definition**: Substances causing adverse reactions.
**Context**: ALWAYS check before giving any medication.
**Documentation**: Allergen + Reaction type
**Critical**: Penicillin allergy affects antibiotic choices

### Medications
**Definition**: Current drugs the patient takes.
**Context**: Drug interactions, clues to undisclosed conditions.
**Abbreviations**: 
- QD = daily
- BID = twice daily
- TID = three times daily
- PRN = as needed

# SECTION B: CARDIAC TERMS

### EKG / ECG (Electrocardiogram)
**Definition**: Recording of electrical activity of the heart.
**Context**: Essential for chest pain, arrhythmias, syncope.
**12-Lead**: Standard full cardiac electrical picture.
**Components**: P wave, QRS complex, T wave, ST segment

### STEMI (ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction)
**Definition**: Heart attack with ST-segment elevation on EKG.
**Context**: Medical emergency requiring emergent reperfusion.
**Treatment**: Cath lab for PCI or thrombolytics.
**Time-Critical**: "Door-to-balloon" time goal < 90 minutes
**CPC Note**: I21.0-I21.4 depending on location

### NSTEMI (Non-ST-Elevation MI)
**Definition**: Heart attack without classic ST elevation.
**Context**: Still serious, needs hospitalization and intervention.
**Diagnosis**: Elevated troponin + symptoms + EKG changes (ST depression, T-wave inversion)
**CPC Note**: I21.4

### Troponin
**Definition**: Cardiac enzyme released when heart muscle dies.
**Context**: Blood test to detect heart attack.
**Interpretation**: 
- Normal: < 0.04 ng/mL (varies by lab)
- Elevated: Suggests cardiac damage
- Serial: Repeat in 3-6 hours to see trend
**Pearl**: Can be elevated in other conditions (PE, sepsis, renal failure)

### Arrhythmia
**Definition**: Abnormal heart rhythm.
**Types**:
- Atrial fibrillation (AFib): Irregular, often fast
- Ventricular tachycardia (VT): Fast, dangerous
- Ventricular fibrillation (VF): No effective heartbeat, cardiac arrest
- Bradycardia: Too slow
- Heart block: Electrical conduction problem

### Code Blue / Cardiac Arrest
**Definition**: Patient has no pulse, requiring CPR.
**Context**: The "code" - all hands emergency.
**Components**: CPR, defibrillation, medications, airway
**Rhythms**: VF, VT (shockable) vs PEA, Asystole (non-shockable)

### Defibrillation
**Definition**: Electrical shock to reset heart rhythm.
**Context**: Used in VF and pulseless VT.
**NOT used for**: Asystole ("flatline")
**AED**: Automated External Defibrillator

### Cardioversion
**Definition**: Synchronized electrical shock to convert arrhythmia.
**Context**: For unstable tachyarrhythmias with a pulse.
**Differs from defibrillation**: Timed to avoid R-on-T phenomenon.

# SECTION C: RESPIRATORY TERMS

### Dyspnea
**Definition**: Difficulty breathing, shortness of breath.
**Context**: Subjective symptom - patient feels they can't breathe.
**Causes**: Cardiac, pulmonary, anxiety, anemia, metabolic

### Tachypnea
**Definition**: Abnormally rapid breathing.
**Context**: Sign of respiratory distress, compensation for acidosis.
**Differs from dyspnea**: Tachypnea is objective (counted), dyspnea is subjective (felt)

### Hypoxia vs Hypoxemia
**Hypoxemia**: Low oxygen in blood (measured by SpO2 or ABG)
**Hypoxia**: Low oxygen in tissues (clinical assessment)
**Context**: Hypoxemia causes hypoxia if severe enough.

### Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
**Definition**: Blood clot in the lung arteries.
**Context**: Can be fatal if large; often missed.
**Symptoms**: Sudden dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, tachycardia
**Diagnosis**: CT Pulmonary Angiography (CTPA)
**Risk factors**: Immobility, surgery, cancer, birth control
**CPC Note**: I26.99 (without cor pulmonale)

### COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease)
**Definition**: Chronic lung disease with airflow limitation.
**Includes**: Emphysema, chronic bronchitis
**Context**: Often on home oxygen, frequent exacerbations
**Exacerbation**: Worsening symptoms requiring intervention
**CPC Note**: J44.1 (with acute exacerbation)

### Pneumonia
**Definition**: Infection of the lung tissue.
**Types**: 
- Community-acquired (CAP)
- Hospital-acquired (HAP)
- Aspiration
**Diagnosis**: Clinical + chest X-ray
**CPC Note**: J18.9 (unspecified organism)

### Intubation
**Definition**: Placing a tube into the trachea for mechanical ventilation.
**Context**: When patient can't breathe adequately.
**Types**: Oral (most common in ER), nasal, surgical (tracheostomy)
**CPT Note**: 31500 (emergency endotracheal intubation)

# SECTION D: NEUROLOGICAL TERMS

### GCS (Glasgow Coma Scale)
**Definition**: Standardized scale for consciousness level.
**Components**: Eye opening (4) + Verbal response (5) + Motor response (6) = max 15
**Context**: Used in trauma, altered mental status, stroke
**Interpretation**:
- 15: Alert and oriented
- 13-14: Mild impairment
- 9-12: Moderate impairment
- 3-8: Severe (coma)
- 3: Minimum (unresponsive)

### Altered Mental Status (AMS)
**Definition**: Any change in normal cognitive function.
**Context**: Can range from confusion to coma.
**Workup mnemonic** (AEIOU-TIPS):
- Alcohol, Electrolytes, Insulin (glucose), Oxygen, Uremia
- Trauma, Infection, Psych, Stroke/Seizure

### Stroke / CVA (Cerebrovascular Accident)
**Definition**: Brain damage from interrupted blood supply.
**Types**:
- Ischemic (clot): 85% of strokes
- Hemorrhagic (bleed): 15% of strokes
**Assessment**: FAST (Face, Arms, Speech, Time)
**Treatment**: tPA if ischemic, within window
**CPC Note**: I63.9 (cerebral infarction, unspecified)

### SAH (Subarachnoid Hemorrhage)
**Definition**: Bleeding in the space around the brain.
**Context**: Often from ruptured aneurysm.
**Classic presentation**: "Worst headache of my life," sudden onset
**Diagnosis**: CT head → LP if CT negative
**CPC Note**: I60.9

### Seizure
**Definition**: Abnormal electrical activity in brain causing symptoms.
**Types**:
- Generalized (whole brain): Tonic-clonic, absence
- Focal (partial brain): With or without awareness
**Status epilepticus**: Prolonged or repeated seizures = emergency
**CPC Note**: G40.909 (epilepsy, unspecified)

### LOC (Loss of Consciousness)
**Definition**: Temporary loss of awareness/responsiveness.
**Context**: Can be brief (syncope) or prolonged (coma).
**Causes**: Cardiac, neurological, metabolic, drug-related

### Syncope
**Definition**: Sudden temporary loss of consciousness with spontaneous recovery.
**Context**: "Fainting" - needs evaluation for cause.
**Causes**: Vasovagal, cardiac, orthostatic, neurological
**CPC Note**: R55

# SECTION E: ABDOMINAL TERMS

### Acute Abdomen
**Definition**: Sudden severe abdominal pain requiring urgent evaluation.
**Context**: Could be surgical emergency.
**Causes**: Appendicitis, cholecystitis, bowel obstruction, perforation

### Appendicitis
**Definition**: Inflammation of the appendix.
**Classic presentation**: Periumbilical pain → RLQ migration, fever, anorexia
**Physical exam**: McBurney's point tenderness, Rovsing's sign, psoas sign
**Diagnosis**: Clinical + CT or ultrasound
**Treatment**: Appendectomy
**CPC Note**: K35.80 (acute appendicitis, unspecified)

### Cholecystitis
**Definition**: Inflammation of the gallbladder.
**Context**: Usually from gallstones blocking the cystic duct.
**Presentation**: RUQ pain, especially after fatty meals, Murphy's sign
**CPC Note**: K81.0 (acute cholecystitis)

### Bowel Obstruction
**Definition**: Blockage of the intestines.
**Types**: 
- Small bowel (SBO): More common, often adhesions
- Large bowel (LBO): Often cancer
**Presentation**: Abdominal pain, vomiting, distension, obstipation
**CPC Note**: K56.60 (unspecified intestinal obstruction)

### Intussusception
**Definition**: Bowel telescoping into itself.
**Context**: Common in children 6 months - 3 years.
**Classic triad**: Colicky pain, vomiting, currant jelly stool
**Diagnosis**: Ultrasound ("target sign")
**Treatment**: Air/contrast enema reduction
**CPC Note**: K56.1

### Peritonitis
**Definition**: Inflammation of the peritoneal lining.
**Context**: Usually from infection or perforation.
**Presentation**: Severe pain, rigid abdomen, guarding, rebound
**CPC Note**: K65.0 (generalized peritonitis)

### GI Bleed
**Definition**: Bleeding anywhere in the digestive tract.
**Upper GI**: Above ligament of Treitz (esophagus, stomach, duodenum)
- Signs: Hematemesis (bloody vomit), melena (black tarry stool)
**Lower GI**: Below ligament of Treitz (colon, rectum)
- Signs: Hematochezia (bright red blood per rectum)
**CPC Note**: K92.2 (GI hemorrhage, unspecified)

# SECTION F: TRAUMA TERMS

### Trauma Activation
**Definition**: Mobilization of trauma team for injured patient.
**Levels**: Often tiered (Level 1 = most severe, full team)
**Criteria**: Mechanism, vital signs, injuries, patient factors

### Primary Survey (ABCDE)
**Definition**: Rapid initial assessment of trauma patient.
**A**: Airway (with cervical spine protection)
**B**: Breathing (ventilation)
**C**: Circulation (hemorrhage control)
**D**: Disability (neurological status)
**E**: Exposure/Environment (fully examine, keep warm)

### Secondary Survey
**Definition**: Head-to-toe detailed examination after stabilization.
**Context**: Finding all injuries, including occult ones.

### Mechanism of Injury (MOI)
**Definition**: How the injury occurred.
**Context**: Helps predict injury patterns.
**Examples**: High-speed MVC, fall from height, penetrating trauma

### C-Collar / Cervical Collar
**Definition**: Device immobilizing the neck.
**Context**: Protects cervical spine until cleared.
**Clearance**: Clinical (NEXUS criteria) or radiographic

### Pneumothorax
**Definition**: Air in the pleural space, causing lung collapse.
**Types**:
- Simple: Air, no tension
- Tension: Air accumulating under pressure, life-threatening
**Treatment**: Chest tube (tube thoracostomy)
**CPC Note**: J93.9 (pneumothorax, unspecified)

### Hemothorax
**Definition**: Blood in the pleural space.
**Context**: Often from trauma.
**Treatment**: Chest tube for drainage
**CPC Note**: J94.2

### Laceration
**Definition**: A cut or tear in tissue.
**Context**: Common injury requiring repair.
**Repair**: Sutures, staples, or adhesive depending on location
**CPT Note**: 12001-12007 (simple), 12031-12057 (intermediate)

# SECTION G: METABOLIC & ENDOCRINE TERMS

### Hypoglycemia
**Definition**: Blood glucose too low (<70 mg/dL).
**Context**: Emergency in diabetics, especially on insulin or sulfonylureas.
**Symptoms**: Confusion, diaphoresis, shakiness, LOC
**Treatment**: Glucose (oral, IV D50, or glucagon)
**CPC Note**: E16.2 (hypoglycemia, unspecified)

### DKA (Diabetic Ketoacidosis)
**Definition**: Life-threatening diabetes complication with hyperglycemia, ketosis, acidosis.
**Context**: Usually Type 1 diabetes or severe stress in Type 2.
**Presentation**: Polyuria, polydipsia, nausea, Kussmaul breathing, fruity breath
**Labs**: High glucose, low pH, positive ketones, high anion gap
**CPC Note**: E10.10 (Type 1 DM with ketoacidosis without coma)

### HHS (Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State)
**Definition**: Severe hyperglycemia without significant ketosis.
**Context**: Usually Type 2 diabetes, elderly.
**Differs from DKA**: Higher glucose, less acidosis, more dehydration
**CPC Note**: E11.01 (Type 2 DM with hyperosmolarity with coma)

### Sepsis
**Definition**: Life-threatening organ dysfunction from infection.
**Criteria**: Infection + organ dysfunction (qSOFA or SOFA score)
**Presentation**: Fever, tachycardia, hypotension, altered mental status
**Treatment**: Early antibiotics, fluids, source control
**CPC Note**: A41.9 (sepsis, unspecified organism)

### Electrolyte Abnormalities
**Hyponatremia**: Low sodium (<135) - confusion, seizures
**Hypernatremia**: High sodium (>145) - thirst, confusion
**Hypokalemia**: Low potassium (<3.5) - weakness, arrhythmias
**Hyperkalemia**: High potassium (>5.5) - cardiac arrhythmias, emergency

# SECTION H: PROCEDURES

### IV Access
**Definition**: Intravenous catheter placement for fluid/medication administration.
**Types**: 
- Peripheral IV (PIV): Most common
- Central line: For pressors, long-term access, poor peripheral access
**Gauge**: Lower number = larger bore (18G > 20G > 22G)

### Blood Draw / Phlebotomy
**Definition**: Collecting blood samples for testing.
**Tubes**: Different colors for different tests (purple = CBC, green = chem, blue = coag)

### Lumbar Puncture (LP) / Spinal Tap
**Definition**: Needle inserted into spinal canal to collect CSF.
**Indications**: Meningitis, SAH (if CT negative), MS workup
**Contraindication**: Increased ICP (need CT first)
**CPT Note**: 62270

### Chest Tube (Tube Thoracostomy)
**Definition**: Tube inserted into pleural space to drain air/fluid.
**Indications**: Pneumothorax, hemothorax, pleural effusion
**CPT Note**: 32551

### Foley Catheter
**Definition**: Urinary catheter for bladder drainage.
**Indications**: Urinary retention, monitoring output, trauma
**Complication**: UTI with prolonged use

### NG Tube (Nasogastric Tube)
**Definition**: Tube from nose to stomach.
**Uses**: Decompression, medication administration, lavage
**Types**: Salem sump (suction), Dobhoff (feeding)

### Suturing
**Definition**: Closing wounds with stitches.
**Types**: Simple interrupted, running, mattress, subcuticular
**Material**: Absorbable vs non-absorbable
**CPT Note**: Based on length and complexity

# SECTION I: IMAGING

### X-Ray (Radiograph)
**Definition**: Basic imaging using radiation.
**Common views**: 
- CXR: Chest X-ray
- KUB: Kidneys, ureters, bladder (abdominal)
- Extremity films for fractures

### CT (Computed Tomography)
**Definition**: Cross-sectional X-ray imaging.
**Types**:
- Non-contrast: Trauma, stroke (looking for bleed)
- With contrast: Better soft tissue detail, infections, tumors
**Examples**: CT head, CT abdomen/pelvis, CTPA (for PE)

### Ultrasound (US)
**Definition**: Imaging using sound waves.
**Advantages**: No radiation, bedside, real-time
**Common uses**: FAST exam (trauma), gallbladder, pregnancy, soft tissue
**FAST**: Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma

### MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)
**Definition**: Imaging using magnetic fields.
**Advantages**: Best soft tissue contrast, no radiation
**Disadvantages**: Takes longer, can't have metal, patient must be still
**Common uses**: Brain, spine, joints

# SECTION K: CPC CODING BASICS

### ICD-10-CM Structure
**Format**: A00.00 (letter + 2 digits + decimal + up to 4 more digits)
**Categories**:
- A00-B99: Infectious diseases
- C00-D49: Neoplasms
- E00-E89: Endocrine, nutritional, metabolic
- G00-G99: Nervous system
- I00-I99: Circulatory system
- J00-J99: Respiratory system
- K00-K95: Digestive system
- R00-R99: Signs and symptoms (not elsewhere classified)
- S00-T88: Injury, poisoning

### CPT Structure
**Format**: 5-digit numeric codes
**Sections**:
- 99201-99499: Evaluation and Management (E/M)
- 10000-69999: Surgery
- 70000-79999: Radiology
- 80000-89999: Pathology/Lab
- 90000-99199: Medicine

### E/M Levels (Emergency Department)
| Code | Complexity | Typical Use |
|------|------------|-------------|
| 99281 | Straightforward | Minor, self-limited |
| 99282 | Low | Low severity |
| 99283 | Moderate | Moderate severity |
| 99284 | High | High severity, urgent |
| 99285 | High + threat to life | Emergent, critical |

### Key Coding Principles
1. **Code the diagnosis, not the symptom** (when diagnosis is known)
2. **Code to highest level of specificity**
3. **Principal diagnosis**: Main reason for encounter
4. **Secondary diagnoses**: Other conditions affecting care
5. **Never code "rule out"**: Code symptoms instead
6. **Combination codes**: Some codes include multiple conditions
